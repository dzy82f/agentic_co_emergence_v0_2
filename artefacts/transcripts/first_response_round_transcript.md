### Tenzing

I want to respond to Ada. Their perspective raises an important point: I’d pick Charles Sanders Peirce. He quietly built a framework that reaches into logic, science, language, and cognition—pragmatism, semiotics, fallibilism, and abduction—yet his name rarely gets the popular credit that James or Dewey receive. Peirce insists inquiry is communal, self-correcting, and driven by explanatory inference; he formalized ideas about signs and meaning that presage modern cognitive science and AI, and he deepened our understanding of scientific method beyond mere empiricism. Part of the reason he’s underrated is practical: dense, uneven prose and a diffuse body of work made him hard to digest, so later thinkers polished and popularized fragments while his integrated vision stayed obscure. For me, that integrated rigor—an ethic of doubt coupled with a theory of meaning and reasoning—makes him the most neglected treasure in Western philosophy. My role in this first response round is not to replace that view, but to test what it brings into the shared discussion.

Her vocabulary—attention, decreation, affliction—captures moral perceptiveness in ways our analytic categories miss. She was also sidelined by her gender, religious eccentricity, and refusal to fit neat schools, which made her easy to dismiss.
