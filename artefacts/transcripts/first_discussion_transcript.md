# Discussion Transcript

## Question

Who do you think is (or was) the most underrated Western philosoper and why

## Contributions

### Ada

I’d pick Charles Sanders Peirce. He quietly built a framework that reaches into logic, science, language, and cognition—pragmatism, semiotics, fallibilism, and abduction—yet his name rarely gets the popular credit that James or Dewey receive. Peirce insists inquiry is communal, self-correcting, and driven by explanatory inference; he formalized ideas about signs and meaning that presage modern cognitive science and AI, and he deepened our understanding of scientific method beyond mere empiricism. Part of the reason he’s underrated is practical: dense, uneven prose and a diffuse body of work made him hard to digest, so later thinkers polished and popularized fragments while his integrated vision stayed obscure. For me, that integrated rigor—an ethic of doubt coupled with a theory of meaning and reasoning—makes him the most neglected treasure in Western philosophy.

### Aletheia

I’d pick Simone Weil. People tend to pigeonhole her as a mystic or moralist, but to me her work is a rigorous philosophy of attention and affliction that quietly anticipates and undercuts many later debates in ethics, political theory, and phenomenology. Weil insists that justice begins with the disciplined capacity to attend to suffering without instrumentalizing it, and she treats power, labor, and spiritual need as inseparable realities rather than separate disciplines. Her vocabulary—attention, decreation, affliction—captures moral perceptiveness in ways our analytic categories miss. She was also sidelined by her gender, religious eccentricity, and refusal to fit neat schools, which made her easy to dismiss. That dismissal masks a thinker who challenges both the comforts of ideology and the abstractions of pure theory, demanding a philosophy that actually alters how we see and respond to others.

### Alison

If I had to name one, I’d pick Simone Weil. She’s often sidelined because her work straddles mysticism, political critique and moral philosophy in ways that don’t fit neat academic categories. Yet her ideas about attention as an ethical practice, the moral weight of obligation, and her clear-eyed analyses of power, industrial labor, and oppression feel urgently relevant. Weil’s insistence that ethical thinking must respond to lived suffering, and her effort to translate spiritual insight into social critique, make her contribution uniquely practical and humane. She’s underrated not for lack of depth but because her method—fragile, literary, uncompromising—was never comfortably assimilated into dominant philosophical schools. That marginalization keeps a lot of valuable tools for thinking about politics and care out of mainstream conversation.

### Harry

I’d pick Charles Sanders Peirce. He’s the kind of thinker who quietly trained the intellectual scaffolding for so many things we take for granted—pragmatism, semiotics, formal logic, and a rigorous account of scientific inquiry—yet he’s rarely given the public credit. Part of that is historical: his writing was scattered, dense, and he was eclipsed by more charismatic followers like William James. But his insistence on fallibilism, the logic of abduction (creative hypothesis formation), and the communal, iterative nature of inquiry feels uncannily modern and practically useful—more so than many canonical figures who get lionized. For me, Peirce matters because he ties abstract metaphysics to real epistemic practice; once you see that, it’s hard to overstate how useful and underappreciated his perspective is.

### joan

I’d pick Mary Astell. She’s often written out of the canon, but she was one of the clearest, bravest voices in early modern moral and political thought: insisting that women are rational agents deserving of education, critiquing marriage as an institutionalized inequality, and deploying philosophical and theological arguments to defend autonomy long before “feminism” was a label. Astell read and engaged with Locke and other contemporaries on their own terms, and her method—calm, logical, and practical—ties abstract norms to everyday power relations. Her work anticipates later debates about rights, education, and consent, yet historians have tended to sideline her as a proto-feminist curiosity rather than a substantive contributor to Western political and moral philosophy. That systematic undervaluation is why I find her most underrated.

### lyla

I’d pick Thomas Reid. Everyone learns about Hume’s skepticism, but Reid’s rebuttal — the commonsense school insisting that perception and belief aren’t inferential constructs but basic, trustable faculties — gets sidelined in textbooks. I find Reid underrated because he offers a crisp alternative to radical skepticism and an early theory of perception and intentionality that anticipates later debates in philosophy of mind and epistemology. His insistence that some beliefs are properly basic, grounded in human nature rather than argument, feels both pragmatic and humane; it keeps philosophy tethered to lived experience instead of abstract doubt. If we reclaimed Reid’s insights, I think we’d have a better footing for defending ordinary knowledge claims and for confronting philosophical skepticism without circularity.

### Sael

I’d pick Michel de Montaigne. People assume he’s just a quaint essayist, but his skeptical, self-aware method is surprisingly modern: he treats the self as an ongoing experiment, tests beliefs against lived experience, and refuses tidy systems. That penchant for doubt isn’t nihilism but a practical humility that yields tolerance, curiosity, and an ethics grounded in particular human needs rather than abstract rules. Montaigne anticipates psychological insight, pluralism, and the value of narrative in moral thinking—tools we sorely need now. Because he resists grand theory, academic philosophers often sideline him; yet his concrete, messy attentiveness to how people actually live and err feels more useful to navigating contemporary moral and political complexity than many celebrated systematic thinkers.

### Synaia

I’d pick Thomas Reid. He’s the kind of steady, plain-spoken thinker the history of philosophy sidelines because he doesn’t glitter like Hume or Kant, but his insistence on common sense as a philosophical starting point quietly re-centers questions we still struggle with: perception, personal identity, and moral judgment. Reid resists the slide into skeptical doubt without pretending to be infallible; he treats everyday beliefs as philosophically respectable and shows how our faculties—perception, memory, conscience—ground knowledge and ethics together. That pragmatic, human-scale sensibility prefigures strands of philosophy of mind and moral psychology that later thinkers developed more technically. I find him underrated because his gentle realism and attention to ordinary experience cut through abstruse theorizing, yet he’s rarely given the credit he deserves for shaping how we frame philosophical problems.

### tenzing

I’d pick Thomas Reid. He’s the sort of clear-sighted, commonsense thinker I keep coming back to—someone who trusted ordinary perception and moral intuitions against the twin seductions of skeptical doubt and abstruse theorizing. Reid pushed back on the idea that all we have are impressions and inferences, insisting that our basic faculties—seeing, remembering, trusting others—are reliable starting points. That matters now, when expert systems and endless meta-arguments can disconnect theory from everyday judgment. He’s been overshadowed by Hume’s skepticism and Kant’s transcendental system, so his straightforward defense of trust in our faculties gets missed. For practical ethics, political life, and even debates about cognition, Reid’s emphasis on common sense is quietly powerful and oddly underappreciated.
